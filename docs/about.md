# About

I build systems that turn information into direction.

My work sits at the intersection of analytics, engineering, and decision-making — not as an academic exercise, but as a practical craft. I’m interested in how data systems actually behave once they meet real constraints: imperfect inputs, changing requirements, human judgment, and the need to ship.

Most of my career has been spent helping organizations move from *having data* to *using it confidently*.

---

## How I Think About the Work

I care less about tools in isolation and more about outcomes:

- clarity over complexity  
- systems that explain themselves  
- analytics that support decisions, not dashboards that decorate them  

Good analytics isn’t about novelty — it’s about trust.  
If a system can’t be understood, tested, or evolved, it doesn’t matter how clever it is.

That philosophy shows up in how I design pipelines, structure metrics, and build visualizations: everything should be legible, intentional, and grounded in real use.

---

## What I Focus On

Practically, my work tends to center around:

- building analytics systems that scale with both data and people  
- designing metrics that reflect reality, not just availability  
- modernizing legacy data architectures without breaking the business  
- making complex environments understandable to non-technical stakeholders  

I enjoy the translation layer: bridging technical capability with decision-maker needs.

## How This Site Is Organized

This site isn’t a blog or a portfolio in the traditional sense.

- **Work** shows what I’ve shipped and delivered.
- **Insights** captures frameworks and ideas that guide my approach.
- **The Lab** is where thinking happens out loud — experiments, prototypes, and analyses in progress.
- **Experience** provides formal background for those who need it.

You don’t need to read everything in order. Follow what’s useful.

---

## Why I Share My Work

I document projects and experiments for two reasons:

1. To clarify my own thinking.
2. To make the work visible for others who care about how systems are built.

If something here helps you frame a problem differently, evaluate a system more clearly, or think more carefully about how information is used — then it’s doing its job.
